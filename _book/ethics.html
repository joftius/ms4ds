4.2 Philosophy of ethics

4.2.1 Meta-ethics

Meta-ethics is about high level questions like:

Can moral statements be factual, or are they just expressions of taste/preference? Are they universal, relative, or meaningless? What approaches can be used to establish moral claims? Can we rely on intuition, reason, or empirical evidence?

We won’t dwell on these questions, rather we’ll proceed under a working assumption that moral claims are meaningful enough to merit our continued study. But we can also remember to reconsider these questions in light of the concepts and examples we focus on below.

4.2.2 Normative ethics

There are a variety of qualitatively different theoretical approaches for how we can determine what is ethical/moral. These approaches are not mutually exclusive so we do not have to choose one, we can and will use a

Consequentialism judges actions based on effects caused by those acts. Most common form of this is utilitarianism: the greatest good (utility) for the greatest number of people. Difficulties in defining utility, trading off between different people, deciding whether to judge specific acts or general rules for guiding actions, and uncertainty about predicting consequences

Deontology judges actions without reference to the effects caused by these actions. Actions can be considered good even if they result in bad consequences as long as they adhere to the correct moral rules

Virtue ethics judges traits. A person can be moral even if they act against deontological rules and their action causes negative consequences, provided the action is in accordance with some deeply held virtue. (Related to psychology? factors attributable to an individual and possibly measured through tests)

Discussion/exercise:

  • Data science is usually framed (perhaps implicitly) as utilitarian because of its focus on prediction/causation (consequences) and optimization (utility). Discuss some prototypical data science problems with explicitly utilitarian language, and indicate how non-consequentialist theories can also apply to these problems.
  • Deontology seems to most obviously relate to “equal treatment” notions of fairness, but (1) can other fairness notions be justified on a deontological basis, (2) how would other ethical theories justify/criticize equal treatment?
  • How do we know/decide which things are virtuous? Is intelligence a virtue? Can different virtues contradict each other, and if so, how can we decide between them?
  • For each of the previous normative frameworks, use that framework to describe an argument (1) supporting the practice of following laws, and (2) supporting the practice of moral law-breaking

4.3 Applied ethics

Applied ethics in data science is the most relevant part of this topic to our course.

Biomedical/health data science: biomedical ethics

Market failure ethics: since the law cannot anticipate and regulate every possible unethical course of action, business decision makers must take responsibility for not exploiting these loopholes. “Self-policing” - does it actually work? (Wall Street was trusted to self-police after deregulation that preceded the 2008 financial crisis).

General data science ethics

  • Statement: Ethical Guidelines for Statistical Practice by the American Statistical Association
  • Statement: Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct by the Association for Computing Machinery
  • Short book: Ethics and Data Science by Mike Loukides, Hilary Mason, and DJ Patil (Patil was Chief Data Scientist during the Obama administration)
  • Book: Data Feminism by Catherine D’Ignazio and Lauren Klein
  • Book: Indigenous Data Sovereignty edited by Tahu Kukutai and John Taylor
  • Case studies: Collection of fictional case studies by the Princeton Dialogues on AI and Ethics

Fairness and causal models

  • Articles: Disparate Causes by Lily Hu
  • Paper: Causal Reasoning for Algorithmic Fairness by Joshua Loftus, Chris Russell, Matt Kusner, and Ricardo Silva
